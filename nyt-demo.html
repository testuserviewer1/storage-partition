Inside a Black Hole

What we know — and what we still don't — about the universe's strangest objects, where gravity overwhelms time and space and the rules of physics begin to fray.

By Jenna Hersh
April 29, 2026

For more than a century, black holes have lived at the strange intersection of theory and observation — first as a mathematical curiosity in Einstein's equations, then as the unseen engines dragging stars and gas into oblivion at the centers of galaxies. Today, with telescopes that can record the silhouette of a black hole and detectors that can hear two of them collide, what was once unimaginable has become almost familiar.

The basic recipe is simple: take an object, then squeeze it into a small enough volume and its gravity becomes inescapable. For the Sun, that critical radius is just under two miles. For Earth, it's a marble. Cross that threshold and the object collapses into a singularity surrounded by an "event horizon" — the boundary beyond which nothing, not even light, can return.

Astronomers now classify black holes by mass. Stellar-mass black holes, a few times heavier than the Sun, form when massive stars exhaust their nuclear fuel and collapse. Supermassive black holes — millions or billions of times the Sun's mass — anchor most galaxies, including our own Milky Way. A still-elusive class of "intermediate-mass" black holes likely fills the gap between them.

The first direct image of a black hole, captured in 2019 by the Event Horizon Telescope, was less a snapshot than a portrait: an orange ring of glowing gas circling a perfect, dark heart. That image, and the more recent picture of Sagittarius A* at the center of our own galaxy, confirmed predictions that physicists had been making with chalk and equations for decades.

Yet the deepest questions remain open. What happens to information that falls past the horizon? How do supermassive black holes grow so large, so quickly, in the early universe? And what, if anything, lies beyond the singularity? Each answer, so far, has only sharpened the next question.
